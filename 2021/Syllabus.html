Language, Cognition, and the Brain

There is nothing easy or simple about language, cognition, or the brain. Language has been argued to be a finite system capable of generating an infinite number of possible combinations. I do not wish to argue this case one way or the other, but even without reaching to infinity, we can probably agree that any natural language can express a variety of thoughts, ideas, emotions, sentiments or observations that is truly staggering. If I did nothing but spend every waking hour for the rest of my life reading books written in my own native language, English, I would die long before I had come close to reading them all, not to mention all the books written in Danish, Spanish, or languages I do not yet understand. At the same time, the brain itself is also composed of a finite number of partially interconnected cells, and yet this finite number of cells is capable of generating all the words in all those books, as well as many multitudes of thoughts, ideas, etc. which have never been recorded in any book, many sentences as yet unsaid, insights not yet had or shared, and so on.

Examining how a piece of our body, the brain, can make all this linguistic magic happen is obviously a formidable task, and we are still far from a good understanding of how it all works. But the vocabulary of cognition and cognitive models gives us a powerful tool for building conceptual bridges between these two very distant conceptual domains. In this course, we will look at neurocognitive models of language and try to build a basic mental model of how language lives and works in our brains, as well as form a basic understanding of the experimental tools we can use to investigate it.

Course Goals

The goal of the course is to help you:

  1. Understand the basic cognitive models of language processes
  2. Understand basic concepts of statistical analysis and experimental design
  3. Interpret the results of psycholinguistic experiments in the context of cognitive models of language processes
  4. Learn to critically evaluate primary and secondary literature in the fields of psycholinguistics and neurolinguistics

What does this mean in practice?

There are two components to the course: Lectures, and Labs.

Lectures

In the lectures, we will discuss some of the current cognitive and neurobiological models of language in the brain. The goal for these lectures is to develop a general framework for understanding how language is “represented” in the brain: how the brain can hear, understand, and produce language. This is a survey course, which means that we will touch briefly on a variety of topics, but will not go into any great depth on any of them. For the lectures, you will need to read, think, and be ready to discuss.

Labs

In the labs, we will divide our time between learning about the basics of statistical analysis, and taking a closer look at how experiments are used to deepen our understanding of the cognitive processes that support language. The point of the labs is to sharpen the critical skills you will need to write your exam papers. For the labs, you will need to have an open mind, and put in some time every week engaging with challenging material. Sometimes this will be fighting with the statistics software, sometimes this will be struggling to understand what a p-value is and why anyone should care, and sometimes it will be seeking to understand what researchers actually did in an experimental report, and what their results mean. My hope is that you will find it all worth it by the end!

Exam

The exam for this course is a short paper on a topic of your choice. The paper can be written individually, or in a group of up to three people. The topic should be in the field of psycholinguistics or neurolinguistics. The name of the course is “Language, Cognition, and the Brain”, and your paper should be about at least two of these things, and one of them has to be language! I highly reccomend you check with me about your topic before committing to it.

Please note that there is a prerequisite for submitting the exam paper. Before you can submit your exam, I have to approve a short (2-4 page) report of a small psycholinguistic experiment that you have conducted, either on your own, or in a group. I strongly reccomend doing these experiments in a group! I will give you more details on how to do this report at the beginning of the semester.
