Chapter 1 Introduction

1.1 What is Language?

What is language? This is one of those questions that seems like it has an easy and obvious answer, but like most questions, the more I think about it, the harder it becomes to give a single, satisfactory answer. Language is something we use every day, so of course we know what it is. It’s how we communicate with each other. Then again, it is not the only way we communicate with each other. If I go to the supermarket, and another shopper and I move to give space to each other in the aisle, we are intentionally communicating information about how we can move our bodies and shopping carts to allow us both to get the items we want from the shelves. But this doesn’t really seem like an example of language. If the same fellow shopper and I misjudge each other’s intentions, and move the wrong way, and smile to each other, this is also communication, but it still doesn’t feel like language.

You can easily think of other examples of humans communicating with other humans in ways that we wouldn’t normally call language: music, painting, photography, dance. If we agree that these are forms of communication, and also agree that they aren’t examples of language, then we must be applying some sort of internal categorical filter that can discriminate linguistic behavior from non-linguistic behavior. We have some sort of internal definition of language that allows us to say that giving a speech, holding a conversation in sign-language, writing an email, reading a novel, and asking a question in class are examples of linguistic behavior, but smiling, raising eyebrows, or making a rude gesture are not.

Then again, although gestures and facial expressions may not exactly be language, they do often accompany linguistic expression, and can often either strengthen or completely negate the sentiment being expressed linguistically. So even those these activities may not examples of linguistic behavior, they are often at least paralinguistic.

Thinking about whether animals can be said to have language can be a very useful way to approach the question of what language is. There is no question that other animals than humans have systems for communicating with each other, and even with humans. My cats have no difficulty communicating to me when they are hungry, and it is easy to see when a dog wants you throw a ball for her to catch. Bees use complex patterns of dance to communicate exact spatial information to each other. Maybe we should include bee dance in our category of linguistic behavior? Hockett and Hockett (1960) enumerate a list of criteria that a system should meet to be considered a language, and consider whether or not bee dance meets these criteria. You may or may not agree with their conclusions. Regardless of where we come down on whether bee dance, dog tail-wagging, or bonobo signing counts as language or not, thinking about these questions forces us to refine our definitions of what language is.

So what is language? We all have an intuitive understanding of what language is, but it is difficult to pin down an exact definition. I have a PhD in Linguistics, and I’m not even sure what language is. Still, it is good to ask ourselves these difficult questions every now and then, maybe once a year. Biologists can’t spend all their time asking themselves what life is; they’d never get anything done. In the same way, linguists need to just assume that they basically know what language is, so they can get on the with business of studying it. But every now and then, asking what “what is” this thing that I study, anyway?" brings a richnheness to our studies that is invaluable, and imbues the daily minutiae of the practice of research with a deeper meaning.

1.2 What is Cognition?

Ok, this book is called “Language, Cognition, and the Brain”. We have already thought about what language is, and decided that, although it can be hard to say exactly what language is, we do kind of know it we see it. So what is cognition, then?

Broadly speaking, cognition means thinking. The word has roots in Greek (gnosis) and Latin (cognoscere). When Descartes’ je pense, donc je suis (1968) was translated to Latin, it became the famous cogito, ergo sum: “I think, therefore I am”. But while Descartes meant considered, rational, and intentional thinking, this is not necessarily what is meant by the modern use of the word “cognition”. It can be all of these things, but not necessarily. Sometimes, “cognitive” is used to mean “not emotional”. Sometimes, “cognitive” has been used to mean “mental”, that is, not just pure instinct. In this sense, the word is meant to emphasize that although things like thoughts and ideas cannot be seen or touched, they can still be thought of as real. We will return to some of these meanings later. Suffice it to say, cognition is related to thought, thinking, and knowledge.

Like most things, the more closely you look at cognition, the more there is to see. A simple example of a cognitive process is vision. There is a cat in front of me, and I see the cat. But vision is not simple at all. “Seeing” the cat involves a long chain of processes, from registering the light reflected from the cats’ fur on my retina, through the transmission of electro-chemical impulses carrying information about the image on the retina along the optic nerve to occipital cortex in my brain, where this information is broken down and analyzed into all of its component parts such as (shape, orientation, color, and motion).

At some point along this chain, the information I have gleaned from the visual information is linked with other kinds of information which I have internally stored: the concept of “cat”, for instance. The light bouncing off of the cat’s fur surely cannot be said to be cognition, but my knowledge that I see a cat in front would count as cognitive, by most definitions. At what point along this chain of information processing does cognition begin? Is the reflection of the cat on my retina an example of cognition? It doesn’t really feel like it; a mirror can reflect an image, but that is not cognition. But most modern accounts of cognition would probably count at least the analysis of shape and color in the visual cortex as cognitive processes. Even though I am not consciously aware that my brain is analyzing the wavelengths of light reflected from the cat’s fur, this analysis involves an internal categorization of external stimuli, which will later be used when I say that “the cat’s fur is sort of orange with a bit of white”.
